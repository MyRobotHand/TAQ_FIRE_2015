Jaime Beechum at Home in Joshua Tree, California

The desert’s physical landscape parallels the psychological state you acquire when coming to this wide, open, arid land and deepens the longer you stay. Nothing lies in the way of your view of the horizon other than the outer edge of the mountain ranges that either break or bring weather. The life that resides in the high desert is abundant, yet camouflaged to the unwilling eye. Each species that survives here—plant, animal, or human—has figured out a way to push through the extremities of climate in order to bask in the fruitfulness that accompanies the hardship.


Here, as in any rural area, one is much closer to the rhythms of the natural cycles and all the elements that culminate to define this space. Daily activities are defined by the hours of daylight, or the lack thereof, or the season (all four exist in this Southern California ecology).


When I first moved to the desert in December 2013, I feared the summer most. Already in a place without water, the temperatures reach 100 degrees without a cloud in the sky to break the blaring sun. This summer, I prepared myself on how to make my way through those fiery months. I would become nocturnal.

As the season changed from spring into summer, and the sun came up a little earlier and brighter, I made the same transition. Eventually I started getting up even before the sun to do chores outdoors or just take care of the morning routine without the resilience of the sun.


I could work until lunch, eat, nap, wake up, have an afternoon coffee and go through another round of work well into the evening and cross into the new hours of the morning. Most of the waking daylight hours were spent indoors, with minimal clothes, or if I was working outside, with long-sleeve linen shirts, shorts, and hat, all drenched with hose water. I would eat endless amounts of melon and drink gallons of water, or anything cold and wet. 


My work site was twenty-five miles west from where I stayed. The early summer mornings took well to the Yamaha XT 250 I puttered across the open mesa into the Canyon most days of the week. The work was physical labor, outdoors. The site: high in the mountains, down dirt roads, and in the boulders that cool the environment to a bearable state. I was working to a close structure that had been established a few years prior. Turns out, the summer months were much nicer to work in than the cold, windy, winter months early in the year. Yet each season was a balance—beauty to bite—teaching self-reliance, strength, and weakness brought out by this desert Earth.
