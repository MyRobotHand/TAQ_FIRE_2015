Machu Picchu

I am a photographer and director. This past summer my friend was working in Lima, Peru so I decided to go see the land of pisco and llamas.

Peru has overwhelming and endless things to see: weavers, giant stone alleys in Cusco, art flea markets in Lima, the grace of Machu Picchu, and miles of golden grasses in the Sacred Valley.

My favorite was the trail up to Intipunku. Same epic views, but away from the crowds. Inti Punku means “sun door” in the local language of Quechua. It is also the name of an Incan Trail connected to Machu Picchu. It was believed that the steps were a control gate for those who enter and exited the Sanctuary.

Sanctuary Lodge: Can’t get any closer to Machu Picchu. Drink a pisco sour with a view of Machu Picchu from the hot tub, seriously.

El Albergue Lodge and Restaurant

El Albergue is a historic and romantic lodge, located in the Ollantaytambo train station platform, about an hour away from Machu Picchu and the city of Cusco. They have their own farm and grow most of the food. This is a slow food style place. Its like Alice Waters went to the Sacred Valley.

Ollantaytambo

Locals call the town, Ollanta. I loved walking around town and exploring the outdoor market at the base of the ruins. On the edge of town look for a path along a stone wall with open farm fields. Go for a sunset walk, local style.

Tree House Restaurant
in Aguas Caliente

Find the Tree House Restaurant up a tiny flight of stairs after wandering down the way from the train tracks. It’s an amazing lunch spot.

Moray

Moray is the name of the Incan agricultural laboratory that was likely used to cultivate resistant and hearty varieties of plants high in the Andes. The area here reminded me of California with the tall golden grasses and rolling hills. The open space is inviting and peaceful.

Honestly, Peru wasn’t on my list of must see places. It seemed like it was going to be too touristy for me. I am a deserted beach kind of gal. I was hesitant. Now… I cannot wait to go back—the desert slams into the ocean, grassy slopes and monochromatic towns with wild colors cracking and popping like purple corn kernels.

The high mountains are calling me.
