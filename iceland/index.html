Photographer

Iceland with Emily Shur

by Cory Jacobs

Robert Adams, really one of the best landscape photographers ever, famously said, “No place is boring, if you’ve had a good night’s sleep and have a pocket full of unexposed film.” It looks like Emily Shur feels the same way. She principally takes pictures of celebrities under the watchful eyes of publicists and large crews at home in Los Angeles. When she is on the road, the landscape is her subject, her fine-art practice the focus. Whether in Japan or New Zealand or New Jersey, Shur makes excellent pictures of places. They have her eye in common: a straight ahead view—not too dissimilar to Adams in fact—and often a hint of humor emerging from an unusual scale shift or a surprising man-made intervention.


Iceland has become a mecca for photo tourism. It’s otherworldly and varied landscape is unlike most other places in the world: tundra, lava fields, glaciers, mountains, fjords, geysers, volcanoes. It’s seductive photographically and almost easy, one could say.


Shur did not go to every place in the guidebook, but took her time at the places she chose. This was her first visit and it was purely for pleasure, (that meaning taking pictures). She stayed focused on scale and feeling. Seeing a small figure in vast landscape “is always such a beautiful reminder of our place in the world. It’s daunting and kind of scary, but also comforting and peaceful. I don’t think I’ve ever been anywhere where scale comes into play more so than in Iceland,” explains Shur. When confronted with an intense cloud of sulphur fumes, she convinced her husband to be engulfed by them—the perfect combination of humor and mystery. Like all good pictures, Shur’s photographs leave you wanting more.
